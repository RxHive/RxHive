# Calcitriol

**Generic Name:** Calcitriol

**Usage:**

Calcitriol is a synthetic form of vitamin D3, a hormone that helps regulate calcium and phosphorus levels in the body.  It's primarily used to treat conditions associated with low blood calcium (hypocalcemia) and low blood phosphorus (hypophosphatemia), such as:

* **Secondary hyperparathyroidism:**  This occurs when the parathyroid glands overproduce parathyroid hormone in response to low calcium levels. Calcitriol helps to suppress this overproduction.
* **Hypocalcemia:** Low calcium levels in the blood, often caused by kidney disease, vitamin D deficiency, or malabsorption.
* **Hypophosphatemia:** Low phosphorus levels in the blood, often associated with kidney disease or malabsorption.
* **Osteoporosis (in some cases):** Though not a first-line treatment, it might be used in certain situations in combination with other therapies.
* **Psoriasis:**  A topical form of Calcitriol is sometimes used to treat psoriasis.

**Side Effects:**

**How it Works:**

Calcitriol binds to vitamin D receptors in the intestines, bones, and kidneys. This binding increases the absorption of calcium and phosphorus from the intestines, promotes calcium release from bones, and reduces the excretion of calcium and phosphorus by the kidneys.  By increasing calcium and phosphorus levels, it helps to regulate parathyroid hormone (PTH) production and improve bone health in individuals with deficient levels.

**FAQs:**

* **Is Calcitriol the same as Vitamin D?** While both are related to vitamin D, Calcitriol is the active form of vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol).  Vitamin D supplements need to be converted in the body to become Calcitriol.
* **How is Calcitriol administered?** Calcitriol is available in oral capsules, tablets, and as an injection. Topical preparations are also available for psoriasis treatment.
* **What are the potential drug interactions?** Calcitriol can interact with various medications, including thiazide diuretics, heart medications (digoxin), and some antacids. It's crucial to inform your doctor about all medications you are taking.
* **Who shouldn't take Calcitriol?** Individuals with hypercalcemia, hyperphosphatemia, kidney stones, or certain heart conditions should generally avoid Calcitriol.  Pregnant or breastfeeding women should consult their doctor before using it.
* **How is Calcitriol monitored?** Blood tests to monitor calcium, phosphorus, and PTH levels are routinely performed to ensure safe and effective treatment.
