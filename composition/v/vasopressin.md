# Vasopressin

**Generic Name:** Vasopressin (also known as antidiuretic hormone or ADH)

**Usage:**

Vasopressin is used to treat:

* **Central diabetes insipidus (CDI):** A condition where the body doesn't produce enough vasopressin, leading to excessive urination and thirst.  Vasopressin replaces the missing hormone.
* **Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone (SIADH):** Ironically, while vasopressin *treats* CDI due to *deficiency*, it is *avoided* in SIADH, where the body produces *too much* vasopressin, leading to fluid retention and low sodium levels.  Treatment in SIADH focuses on managing fluid intake and correcting sodium imbalances.
* **Gastrointestinal bleeding:**  High doses of vasopressin can constrict blood vessels in the gastrointestinal tract, helping to control bleeding (though other methods are often preferred).
* **Cardiac arrest (in some cases):**  In certain situations of cardiac arrest, vasopressin may be used to improve blood pressure and heart function, although epinephrine is usually the first-line treatment.
* **Hypotension (low blood pressure) associated with septic shock:**  Vasopressin may be used as an adjunct to other treatments to raise blood pressure, particularly in cases where other medications aren't effective.


**Side Effects:**

Side effects can vary depending on the dosage and route of administration, but common ones include:

* **Water intoxication (hyponatremia):**  The body retains too much water, leading to low sodium levels.  This is particularly a concern with higher doses and in patients with impaired kidney function. Symptoms include headache, nausea, vomiting, confusion, and seizures.
* **Increased blood pressure:** Vasopressin constricts blood vessels, so this can be a significant side effect, especially in patients with hypertension.

**How it Works:**

Vasopressin is a hormone naturally produced by the body in the hypothalamus and stored in the posterior pituitary gland. Its primary function is to regulate water balance by increasing the reabsorption of water in the kidneys. This reduces urine production and helps to maintain normal blood pressure and fluid balance.  It also has effects on blood vessel constriction (vasoconstriction).  Different receptors in the body respond to vasopressin in various ways, leading to its diverse effects.
