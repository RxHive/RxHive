# Lidocaine + Prilocaine
### Generic Name
Lidocaine + Prilocaine

### Usage
Lidocaine and prilocaine is a combination topical anesthetic used primarily for pain relief during minor medical procedures.  Its primary use is for surface anesthesia prior to injections, minor surgical procedures, or other minimally invasive interventions.  This combination is frequently used in dermatological procedures like biopsies, lesion removal, and wound care. It's also employed for managing pain associated with superficial wounds or burns.  While not its primary indication, it can provide temporary relief from certain types of localized pain, like that from insect bites or minor cuts.  It's important to note that this combination is for superficial use only and is not intended for deep anesthesia or internal use.

### Dosage
Dosage varies greatly depending on the specific application, the size of the area to be treated, and the patient's age and overall health.  There isn't a standard dosage across all uses.  A healthcare professional will determine the appropriate amount needed.  The medication is typically applied topically as a cream, ointment, or patch.  It's crucial to follow the instructions provided by the physician or pharmacist precisely.  

* **Adults:** The amount applied will be determined by the healthcare provider based on the area being treated. It is generally applied to the skin in a thin layer, and the duration of application will depend on the procedure.  
* **Children:**  The use of lidocaine and prilocaine in children requires careful consideration and should always be under the supervision of a healthcare professional.  The dosage will be significantly lower than for adults and carefully tailored to the child's age, weight, and the area being treated.
* **Oral and IV administration:** This combination is *not* intended for oral or intravenous administration.  It is exclusively for topical application.


### Side Effects
Common side effects are generally mild and localized, potentially including:

* Burning or stinging sensation at the application site.
* Skin redness or irritation.
* Swelling at the application site.
* Temporary discoloration of the skin.

Less common, but more serious side effects are rare but should be reported immediately to a doctor:

* Allergic reactions (rash, itching, hives, difficulty breathing).
* Methemoglobinemia (a condition where the blood's ability to carry oxygen is reduced).  This is more likely with larger application areas or in patients with certain underlying conditions.
* Systemic absorption leading to cardiovascular or neurological effects (rare, especially with proper application).


If any adverse effects occur, discontinue use and seek medical attention.


### How it Works
Lidocaine and prilocaine are both amide-type local anesthetics.  They work by blocking nerve signals that transmit pain sensations from the treated area to the brain.  Specifically, they bind to sodium channels in nerve cell membranes, preventing sodium ions from entering the cells. This disruption of sodium channels prevents the propagation of nerve impulses, thus providing local pain relief. The combination provides a synergistic effect, meaning that their combined anesthetic effect is greater than the sum of their individual effects.

### Precautions
* **Allergy:** Patients with a known allergy to lidocaine, prilocaine, or other amide-type local anesthetics should avoid this medication.
* **Pregnant or breastfeeding women:**  Use during pregnancy and breastfeeding should be carefully considered and only under the guidance of a healthcare professional.  The benefits must outweigh the potential risks.
* **Children:**  Use in children requires careful supervision by a healthcare professional.
* **Skin conditions:** Avoid application to damaged or irritated skin.
* **Large application areas:**  Application to extensive areas of skin increases the risk of systemic absorption and potential side effects.
* **Drug interactions:** Although rare, interactions with other medications are possible.  Inform your doctor about all medications you are currently taking.
* **Methemoglobinemia risk factors:** Individuals with certain enzyme deficiencies or those taking certain medications are at a higher risk of developing methemoglobinemia.


### FAQs

* **Q: How long does the numbing effect last?** A: The duration of the numbing effect varies depending on the concentration, the amount applied, and the specific area treated.  It generally lasts from 30 minutes to several hours.

* **Q: Can I use this on open wounds?** A:  It's generally not recommended to apply to open wounds without consulting a healthcare professional.

* **Q: How should I store this medication?** A: Store as directed on the product label. Typically this involves keeping it at room temperature, away from heat and moisture.

* **Q: Is it safe to use lidocaine and prilocaine near the eyes or mucous membranes?** A:  Avoid contact with eyes and mucous membranes.  If accidental contact occurs, rinse thoroughly with water.

* **Q: What should I do if I experience a serious side effect?** A: Stop using the medication immediately and seek medical attention.
