# Policosanol
### Generic Name
Policosanol

### Usage
Policosanol is primarily used to lower cholesterol levels in individuals with type II hypercholesterolemia (high cholesterol) and dyslipidemia (abnormal lipid levels) often associated with type 2 diabetes.  It's also shown promise in improving symptoms of intermittent claudication (pain in the legs due to reduced blood flow).  Research suggests it may help reduce smooth muscle proliferation, potentially benefiting those with cardiovascular disease.  Some studies indicate that when combined with omega-3 fatty acids, policosanol may improve mood and reaction time in athletes, although more research is needed in this area.  It's crucial to remember that policosanol shouldn't replace other established cholesterol-lowering treatments.

### Dosage
**Adult Dosage (Hypercholesterolemia):**  Policosanol is typically administered orally.  The starting dose is usually 5 mg per day, which may be gradually increased to a maximum of 20 mg per day, depending on individual response and physician guidance. In some cases, after percutaneous stent implantation, a higher dose of 40 mg/day for 30 days may be used in conjunction with antiplatelet medications.

**Pediatric Dosage:** The safety and efficacy of policosanol in children have not been established.  It should not be given to children.

### Side Effects
Common side effects, while not precisely quantified in terms of frequency, may include:

*   Rash
*   Fatigue
*   Headache
*   Weight loss
*   Increased urination
*   Insomnia

While less common, serious side effects are possible.  It's essential to report any unusual or concerning symptoms to a healthcare provider immediately.

### How it Works
Policosanol primarily lowers cholesterol by impacting the synthesis and breakdown of cholesterol in the body. Specifically, it's thought to inhibit the enzyme 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl coenzyme A (HMG CoA) reductase, a key enzyme in cholesterol production.  Additionally, it's believed to reduce platelet aggregation (clumping) by decreasing the production of thromboxane B2, a substance that promotes blood clotting.  These actions contribute to its cholesterol-lowering and potentially cardiovascular-protective effects.

### Precautions
*   **Drug Interactions:**  Because policosanol may affect platelet aggregation, caution is advised when used concurrently with anticoagulants (like warfarin) or antiplatelet agents (such as aspirin, clopidogrel, or prasugrel).  Always inform your doctor of all medications you are taking.
*   **Pregnancy and Lactation:**  Information regarding the safety and efficacy of policosanol during pregnancy and breastfeeding is limited.  It should be avoided unless specifically recommended by a healthcare professional.
*   **Other Considerations:** Policosanol is generally not recommended as a first-line treatment for high cholesterol. It's more often considered as an adjunct therapy or for patients who cannot tolerate other lipid-lowering medications.  It's best taken before bedtime.
