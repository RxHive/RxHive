# Phenindione

**Generic Name:** Phenindione

**Usage:** Phenindione is an anticoagulant medication used to prevent and treat blood clots.  It's primarily used in the long-term management of venous thromboembolism (VTE), including deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE), and to prevent further clots in individuals with a history of these conditions.  It may also be used after certain types of surgery to reduce the risk of blood clot formation.  **However, it's important to note that phenindione is less commonly used than other anticoagulants like warfarin and newer direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) due to its higher risk of side effects and the need for frequent monitoring.**

**Side Effects:**  Phenindione carries a significant risk of side effects, some of which can be serious.  These include:

* **Bleeding:** This is the most common and serious side effect, ranging from minor bruising and bleeding gums to severe internal bleeding.
* **Gastrointestinal problems:** Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea.
* **Liver damage:** Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes), abnormal liver function tests.
* **Allergic reactions:** Skin rashes, itching, hives.
* **Agranulocytosis:** A severe decrease in white blood cells, increasing the risk of infection.
* **Purpura:** Purple spots on the skin due to bleeding under the skin.
* **Nephropathy (Kidney problems):** Although rare.


**How it Works:** Phenindione is a vitamin K antagonist.  It works by interfering with the liver's ability to produce vitamin K-dependent clotting factors (factors II, VII, IX, and X). By reducing the production of these factors, phenindione inhibits blood clot formation.

* **Is phenindione better than other anticoagulants?** No. While effective, phenindione has a higher risk of side effects and requires more frequent monitoring than other anticoagulants like warfarin and DOACs.  Its use is generally reserved for situations where other anticoagulants are contraindicated or ineffective.

* **How often do I need blood tests?**  Regular blood tests (prothrombin time/INR) are crucial to monitor the effectiveness of phenindione and adjust the dosage to maintain the desired therapeutic range. The frequency of testing varies depending on individual needs and physician recommendations but is typically more frequent than for warfarin.

* **What should I do if I miss a dose?**  Contact your doctor immediately to determine the appropriate course of action.  Do not double the next dose.

* **What interactions does phenindione have with other medications?** Phenindione interacts with many medications, including some over-the-counter drugs. It's crucial to inform your doctor and pharmacist about all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies you are taking.

* **What are the signs of bleeding while on phenindione?**  Signs of bleeding can range from minor bruising and bleeding gums to more serious symptoms like black or bloody stools, blood in the urine, unusually heavy menstrual bleeding, dizziness, and fainting.
