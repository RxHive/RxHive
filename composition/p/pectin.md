# Pectin
### Generic Name
Pectin

### Usage
Pectin is primarily used as an antidiarrheal agent. It works by adsorbing irritants in the intestinal tract, thus soothing the intestinal mucosa and helping to relieve diarrhea symptoms.  Beyond its antidiarrheal properties, pectin has also been studied for its potential effects on various other conditions, including cancer, diabetes, and gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)/gastric ulceration. However, more research is needed to definitively establish its efficacy in these areas.  Its use as a demulcent (soothing agent) for mucosal irritation is also recognized.

### Dosage
Dosage varies depending on the intended use and the patient's age.  Always follow the instructions on the product label or as directed by a healthcare professional.

**Diarrhea (Adult):**  10-20 grams per day orally.

**Mucosal Irritation/Demulcent (Adult):**  For lozenges containing pectin-methanol, the typical dosage is 5 mg repeated hourly, up to a maximum of 10 lozenges per day.

**Cholesterol Reduction (Adult):** 10-20 mg/kg/day orally.  Note that the efficacy of pectin for cholesterol reduction is still under investigation.

**Pediatric Use (Mucosal Irritation/Demulcent):**  The safety and efficacy of pectin in children under 3 years of age have not been established. For children older than 3, the dosage may be the same as for adults, but always consult a pediatrician before administering to children.

**Dosage Adjustments:**  Currently, there are no specific dosage adjustments recommended by manufacturers for patients with hepatic (liver) or renal (kidney) impairment.

### Side Effects
Common side effects are generally mild and infrequent with pectin use at recommended doses. However, potential side effects include:

*   Gastrointestinal upset (mild)
*   Constipation (in some individuals)

Serious side effects are rare but possible, especially with high doses.  Consult a doctor immediately if you experience:

* Severe gastrointestinal discomfort
* Allergic reactions (rash, itching, swelling)


### How it Works
Pectin is a soluble fiber that works primarily by adsorbing irritants and toxins within the gastrointestinal tract.  This adsorption helps to bind these substances, preventing them from further irritating the intestinal lining.  The pectin also helps to increase stool bulk, which can aid in the relief of diarrhea. Its demulcent properties further contribute to soothing irritated mucosa.  The mechanism of its potential effects on conditions like cancer and diabetes is still under investigation.

### Precautions
* **Contraindications:** Pectin should not be used by individuals with known hypersensitivity or allergy to pectin.
* **Drug Interactions:** Pectin may decrease the effectiveness of certain medications, including tetracyclines, digoxin, and lovastatin.  Concomitant use is not recommended. Consult your physician or pharmacist about potential interactions with other medications you are taking.
* **Warnings:**  Very high doses of pectin (50-100 mg/kg) may increase the risk of gastric mucosal lesions.  Use caution and consult a healthcare professional. Lozenges should be used cautiously in young children to avoid choking hazards.
* **Pregnancy and Lactation:**  Pectin is considered possibly safe in pregnant and breastfeeding women when used in amounts typically found in food.  Larger medicinal doses may be safe, but always consult a healthcare professional before using during pregnancy or breastfeeding.  It is excreted in breast milk.
