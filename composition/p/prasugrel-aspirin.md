# Prasugrel + Aspirin

**Generic Name:** Prasugrel/Aspirin (Note:  There isn't a single FDA-approved generic combination product of prasugrel and aspirin.  Prasugrel and aspirin are often prescribed together, but as separate medications.)

**Usage:**  Prasugrel and aspirin are used together in the treatment and prevention of cardiovascular events, specifically after a heart attack (myocardial infarction) or in patients with acute coronary syndrome (ACS).  The combination aims to reduce the risk of blood clots forming in arteries.  Aspirin helps prevent blood clot formation while prasugrel acts more powerfully to inhibit platelet aggregation (platelets sticking together), a crucial step in clot formation.  This combination is typically prescribed for patients undergoing percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI).  **The use of prasugrel and aspirin is only on the prescription of a physician and the dosage is highly individualized.**

    * Dizziness
    * Headache
    * Nausea
    * Diarrhea

* **Aspirin:**
    * Gastrointestinal problems: Stomach upset, heartburn, ulcers, bleeding.
    * Allergic reactions:  Rash, itching, swelling.
    * Increased risk of bleeding (especially when combined with prasugrel).
    * Tinnitus (ringing in the ears)


**How it Works:**

* **Prasugrel:**  Is a thienopyridine, a type of antiplatelet drug. It works by irreversibly blocking the P2Y12 receptor on platelets, preventing platelet aggregation and reducing the risk of blood clot formation.  It's significantly more potent than clopidogrel.
* **Aspirin:**  Is a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) that inhibits the production of thromboxane A2, a substance that promotes platelet aggregation and vasoconstriction. It also has anti-inflammatory and analgesic (pain-relieving) effects, though these are not the primary reasons for its use in this context.

The combination of prasugrel and aspirin provides a more comprehensive approach to antiplatelet therapy, offering stronger inhibition of platelet aggregation than aspirin alone.
