# Prothionamide

**Generic Name:** Prothionamide

**Usage:** Prothionamide is an antituberculosis drug.  It's a second-line agent, meaning it's typically used when first-line drugs (like isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol) are ineffective or when the patient has developed resistance to them.  It's often used in combination with other antituberculosis medications to treat multi-drug resistant tuberculosis (MDR-TB) and extensively drug-resistant tuberculosis (XDR-TB).

**Side Effects:** Prothionamide has a significant number of potential side effects, some of which can be serious. These include:

* **Hepatotoxicity (liver damage):** This is a major concern and necessitates regular liver function tests during treatment.  Symptoms can range from mild fatigue to jaundice and liver failure.
* **Gastrointestinal disturbances:** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, anorexia (loss of appetite).
* **Neurological effects:** Peripheral neuropathy (numbness, tingling, pain in the extremities), dizziness, headache, tremors, psychosis.
* **Skin reactions:** Rash, itching, photosensitivity (increased sensitivity to sunlight).
* **Thyroid dysfunction:** Hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid) or, less commonly, hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid).
* **Drug interactions:** Prothionamide can interact with other medications, potentially increasing or decreasing their effectiveness or side effects.  This is a significant concern given it's typically used in combination therapy.


**How it Works:** Prothionamide is a thioamide derivative that inhibits the synthesis of mycolic acids, essential components of the Mycobacterium tuberculosis cell wall. By disrupting cell wall synthesis, it prevents the growth and replication of the bacteria.  The exact mechanism of action isn't fully understood, but it's believed to interfere with various metabolic processes within the bacteria.
