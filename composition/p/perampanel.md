# Perampanel

**Generic Name:** Perampanel

**Usage:**

Perampanel is an anticonvulsant medication used as an adjunctive therapy (meaning it's used in addition to other medications) in the treatment of:

* **Partial-onset seizures** in adults with epilepsy. This includes seizures with or without secondary generalization (meaning the seizure spreads to involve the whole brain).
* **Myoclonic seizures** in adults with juvenile myoclonic epilepsy.

It is *not* a first-line treatment for epilepsy and is usually prescribed when other medications haven't been effective enough.  It's important to note that perampanel does not cure epilepsy; it helps to manage and reduce the frequency and severity of seizures.

**Side Effects:**

Perampanel, like other anticonvulsants, can cause a range of side effects, varying in severity from person to person.  Some common side effects include:

* **Dizziness:** This is a very common side effect and can increase the risk of falls.
* **Drowsiness:**  This can impact daily activities and driving ability.
* **Headache:**  Often mild to moderate.
* **Fatigue:** Feeling tired and lacking energy.
* **Irritability:**  Increased moodiness or frustration.
* **Difficulty with balance and coordination:**  Increased risk of falls.
* **Nausea and vomiting:** Gastrointestinal upset.
* **Weight gain:**  Can be significant in some individuals.
* **Psychiatric effects:**  These are less common but potentially serious and can include depression, anxiety, aggression, hostility, hallucinations, and suicidal ideation.


**Serious side effects, though less common, require immediate medical attention and include:**

* **Severe allergic reactions:**  Symptoms like rash, hives, swelling, difficulty breathing.
* **Suicidal thoughts or actions:**  This is a serious risk and requires immediate intervention.
* **Severe dizziness or fainting:**  Increased risk of falls and injury.


**How it Works:**

Perampanel works by selectively blocking AMPA receptors in the brain.  AMPA receptors are involved in the transmission of excitatory signals between nerve cells. By blocking these receptors, perampanel reduces the excessive neuronal activity that contributes to seizures.  The exact mechanism by which it reduces seizure frequency is still not fully understood.
