# Opipramol
### Generic Name
Opipramol

### Usage
Opipramol is primarily used to treat generalized anxiety disorders and somatoform disorders.  These are conditions characterized by excessive worry and anxiety, often accompanied by physical symptoms that are not fully explained by a medical condition.  While not its primary indication, it may be used off-label in certain circumstances, but this should only be done under the strict guidance of a physician.  It's crucial to remember that Opipramol is not a first-line treatment for these conditions and other therapies might be considered first.

### Dosage

**Adults:** The typical starting dose for generalized anxiety disorder and somatoform disorders is 50 mg in the morning, 50 mg in the evening, and 100 mg at night.  This can be adjusted based on individual response and tolerance.  The dose can be lowered to 50-100 mg once daily (preferably at night) or increased up to 300 mg per day, divided into three doses, depending on effectiveness and how well the patient tolerates the medication. Always follow your doctor's specific instructions.

**Children:** The safety and efficacy of Opipramol in children have not been established. It should not be given to children.

**Dosage Adjustments:**  Dosage adjustments are necessary for patients with hepatic (liver) or renal (kidney) impairment.  For liver impairment, the manufacturer's labeling does not provide specific adjustments, and use is contraindicated in cases of severe impairment.  For kidney impairment, a dose reduction is recommended, though the exact amount is not specified in the labeling.  A doctor will guide adjustments based on the individual's condition.

### Side Effects

Common side effects may include:

* Dry mouth
* Stuffy nose
* Fatigue
* Drowsiness
* Dizziness
* Increased sweating
* Blurred vision
* Speech impairment
* Tremors
* Heart rhythm disturbances
* Palpitations
* Orthostatic hypotension (dizziness upon standing)
* Liver dysfunction
* Constipation
* Weight gain
* Urinary retention
* Decreased libido (sex drive)
* Increased thirst
* Rash
* Urticaria (hives)


Less common but more serious side effects can occur.  If you experience any unusual symptoms or side effects, contact your doctor immediately.

### How it Works

Opipramol is a tricyclic antidepressant (TCA).  While the exact mechanism isn't fully understood, it's believed to work by affecting various neurotransmitters in the brain, including serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine.  It acts as an antagonist at several receptors, including D2, 5-HT2, H1, H2, and muscarinic acetylcholine receptors.  The antagonism of the H1 and H2 receptors explains its antihistamine effects, and the muscarinic acetylcholine receptor antagonism accounts for its anticholinergic properties. It's important to note that Opipramol exhibits strong antihistaminic effects but weak anticholinergic and antiadrenergic effects compared to other TCAs.  This unique profile contributes to its therapeutic effects in anxiety and somatoform disorders.

### Precautions

* **Contraindications:** Opipramol is contraindicated (should not be used) in individuals with hypersensitivity to Opipramol or other TCAs, alcohol or barbiturate intoxication, benign prostatic hyperplasia and urinary retention, glaucoma, pyloric stenosis, bowel obstruction, predisposition to seizures, severe hepatic or renal impairment, acute delirium, pre-existing higher-degree AV block, or diffuse supraventricular or ventricular conduction disorders.  It should also not be used in combination with MAO inhibitors.
* **Drug Interactions:** Opipramol can interact with several other medications.  It reduces the hypotensive effects of guanethidine and clonidine, may cause additive CNS depression with alcohol or other CNS depressants, increases the effects of cimetidine, phenothiazines, haloperidol, SSRIs, and oral anticoagulants, increases the risk of arrhythmias with quinidine, exhibits additive toxicity with other anticholinergics, and has potentially fatal interactions with MAO inhibitors.  Always inform your doctor of all medications you are taking.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** Opipramol is categorized as pregnancy category C.  It should only be used during pregnancy if the potential benefits outweigh the risks.  It's also excreted in breast milk and breastfeeding is not recommended while taking Opipramol.
* **Other Precautions:** Use caution in patients with bradycardia, cardiac arrhythmias, or hypokalemia.  Monitor liver function tests (LFTs) during prolonged treatment.


### FAQs

* **Q: Can I drive while taking Opipramol?** A:  No, avoid driving or operating heavy machinery if you experience dizziness or drowsiness.

* **Q: Can I drink alcohol while taking Opipramol?** A: No, avoid alcohol consumption while on Opipramol as it can enhance the CNS depressant effects.

* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it is close to the time for your next dose. Never double up on doses.

* **Q: How should I store Opipramol?** A: Store Opipramol as directed on the label, typically at room temperature and away from moisture and light.

* **Q: Can I stop taking Opipramol suddenly?** A: No, abrupt discontinuation can lead to a discontinuation syndrome.  Always consult your doctor before stopping Opipramol.  They will help you gradually reduce your dose to minimize withdrawal effects.
