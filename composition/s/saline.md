# Saline

**Generic Name:** Sodium Chloride (NaCl)

**Usage:**  Sodium chloride solution (saline) has various uses depending on the concentration and method of administration.  Common uses include:

* **Fluid resuscitation:**  Replacing fluids lost through dehydration, vomiting, diarrhea, or excessive sweating (typically administered intravenously).
* **Irrigation:** Cleaning wounds, irrigating nasal passages, and during surgical procedures.
* **Electrolyte replacement:**  Replenishing sodium and chloride levels in the body (intravenously).
* **Inhalation:**  Used in nebulizers to treat respiratory conditions like cystic fibrosis.
* **Contact lens solution:**  Lubricating and cleaning contact lenses.
* **Eye wash:**  Rinsing the eyes of irritants.


**Side Effects:** Side effects are generally mild and depend heavily on the route of administration and concentration.  Possible side effects include:

* **Intravenous administration:**  Fluid overload (edema, shortness of breath), heart failure exacerbation, electrolyte imbalances (hyponatremia, hyperchloremia), and phlebitis (inflammation of the vein).  Rarely, more serious reactions can occur.
* **Topical application (eye, nose, wound):**  Burning, stinging, temporary discomfort.  Allergic reactions are rare.
* **Inhalation:** Coughing, wheezing (though usually related to the underlying respiratory condition).


**How it Works:** Saline works by providing sodium and chloride ions, essential electrolytes, to the body.  These ions are crucial for maintaining fluid balance, nerve and muscle function, and many other bodily processes. When administered intravenously, it increases blood volume and helps restore electrolyte balance.  When used topically, it helps cleanse and flush out irritants or debris.  Its effectiveness as an irrigation solution is due to its isotonicity (similar salt concentration to the body's fluids), preventing damage to cells.
