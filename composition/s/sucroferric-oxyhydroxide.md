# Sucroferric oxyhydroxide

**Generic Name:** Sucroferric oxyhydroxide

**Usage:**

Sucroferric oxyhydroxide is a phosphate binder used to control serum phosphorus levels in adult patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) on dialysis.  It's specifically indicated for patients with hyperphosphatemia (high phosphorus levels in the blood) who are at risk for complications associated with this condition, such as bone disease and cardiovascular problems.  It's usually prescribed as part of a broader management strategy for CKD, which may also include diet modification and other medications.

**Side Effects:**

The most common side effects of sucroferric oxyhydroxide include:

* **Gastrointestinal effects:**  These are the most frequent and can include constipation, diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and dark-colored stools (due to the iron content).
* **Hypotension (low blood pressure):** Although less common, this is a potential side effect, particularly in patients already prone to hypotension.
* **Hypophosphatemia (low phosphorus levels):** While the goal is to reduce high phosphorus, over-treatment can lead to low phosphorus levels.  This is usually manageable with adjustments to dosage.
* **Other rare side effects:**  These include allergic reactions (rash, itching, swelling), changes in liver function tests, and discoloration of teeth.

It's important to note that the severity and frequency of side effects vary among individuals.


**How it Works:**

Sucroferric oxyhydroxide works by binding to phosphate in the gastrointestinal tract, preventing its absorption into the bloodstream.  This reduces the amount of phosphorus that enters the body, thus lowering serum phosphorus levels.  The bound phosphate is then eliminated from the body through the stool.  The sucrose component enhances the palatability and helps with the absorption of iron, which is an added benefit for CKD patients often suffering from iron deficiency anemia.
