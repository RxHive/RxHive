# Semaglutide
### Generic Name
Semaglutide

### Usage
Semaglutide is primarily used to improve glycemic control in adults with type 2 diabetes mellitus.  It's administered as an adjunct to diet and exercise, meaning it works best when combined with lifestyle changes.  Importantly, injectable semaglutide is also approved to reduce the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events in adults with type 2 diabetes and established cardiovascular disease.  Beyond diabetes management, semaglutide is indicated for chronic weight management in adults with a body mass index (BMI) of 30 kg/m² or greater (obesity), or 27 kg/m² or greater (overweight) if they also have at least one weight-related comorbid condition (like hypertension, type 2 diabetes, or dyslipidemia).  It's used in conjunction with a reduced-calorie diet and increased physical activity.

### Dosage
Semaglutide is available in both injectable (subcutaneous) and oral formulations.  Dosage varies significantly depending on the formulation and the reason for use (diabetes or weight management).  

**Injectable Semaglutide (Subcutaneous):**

* **Type 2 Diabetes:**  Typically starts at 0.25 mg once weekly for 4 weeks, then may be increased to 0.5 mg and eventually 1 mg once weekly, depending on individual response and glycemic control.  The maximum dose is usually 1 mg weekly.
* **Weight Management:** Begins with 0.25 mg once weekly, gradually increasing over several weeks to a maintenance dose of 2.4 mg once weekly.  This gradual increase helps minimize gastrointestinal side effects.  Dosage adjustments may be necessary based on individual tolerability.

**Oral Semaglutide:**

* **Type 2 Diabetes:** The initial dose is 3 mg once daily for 30 days, which may be increased to 7 mg and then 14 mg daily as needed to reach glycemic goals.  The lower starting dose aims to reduce gastrointestinal issues.

**Important Considerations:**

* **Missed Doses:**  Instructions for handling missed doses vary between the injectable and oral forms and also depend on whether it is being used for diabetes or weight management.  Always refer to your prescription instructions or consult your doctor.
* **Conversion Between Formulations:**  Switching between oral and injectable semaglutide requires careful dose adjustments under the guidance of a healthcare provider.


### Side Effects
Common side effects, especially with initial use, often involve the gastrointestinal system and can include:

* Nausea
* Diarrhea
* Vomiting
* Constipation
* Abdominal pain
* Decreased appetite

Less common but more serious side effects can include:

* Pancreatitis (inflammation of the pancreas)
* Gallbladder problems
* Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) – particularly when used with insulin or other diabetes medications
* Allergic reactions (ranging from rash to severe anaphylaxis)
* Thyroid C-cell tumors (rare but a serious concern, see Precautions)
* Acute kidney injury


If you experience any of these side effects, especially the more serious ones, contact your healthcare provider immediately.

### How it Works
Semaglutide is a glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor agonist.  It mimics the effects of GLP-1, a natural hormone that helps regulate blood sugar.  Semaglutide works in several ways:

* **Increased Insulin Secretion:** It stimulates the release of insulin from the pancreas, particularly when blood sugar levels are high.
* **Decreased Glucagon Secretion:** It suppresses the release of glucagon, a hormone that raises blood sugar levels.
* **Delayed Gastric Emptying:** This slows down the rate at which food leaves the stomach, contributing to better blood sugar control.

For weight management, semaglutide also affects appetite regulation in the brain, potentially leading to reduced food intake.

### Precautions
* **Thyroid Tumors:** Semaglutide has been associated with thyroid C-cell tumors in animal studies. While the relevance to humans is unclear, it's contraindicated in individuals with a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) or multiple endocrine neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2).  Regular monitoring for thyroid problems is not routinely recommended.
* **Pancreatitis:**  There is an increased risk of pancreatitis, so monitor for symptoms like severe abdominal pain.
* **Hypersensitivity:**  Semaglutide can cause allergic reactions, ranging from mild to severe.  Those with a history of severe allergic reactions to other GLP-1 receptor agonists should use caution.
* **Kidney and Gallbladder Problems:**  There's an increased risk of kidney and gallbladder problems.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  Semaglutide should generally be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to potential risks to the developing fetus and infant.  Consult your doctor if you are pregnant, breastfeeding, or planning to become pregnant.
* **Drug Interactions:** Semaglutide can interact with other medications, especially insulin and insulin secretagogues (which can increase the risk of hypoglycemia), and certain chemotherapy drugs. Always inform your doctor of all medications you are taking.
* **Suicidal Thoughts:** There are reports linking GLP-1 receptor agonists to suicidal thoughts or behavior. If you experience these, contact your doctor immediately.
