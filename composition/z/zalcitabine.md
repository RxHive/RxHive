# Zalcitabine
### Generic Name
Zalcitabine

### Usage
Zalcitabine is a nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor (NRTI) that was used in the treatment of HIV/AIDS.  It works by interfering with the action of reverse transcriptase, an enzyme HIV uses to replicate itself.  However, it's crucial to understand that due to the rapid emergence of viral resistance when used alone, zalcitabine is no longer widely used and has been discontinued in most markets.  It was primarily used in combination with other antiretroviral medications.

### Dosage
Because zalcitabine is largely discontinued, providing specific dosage information is not recommended as it should only be used under strict medical supervision by a healthcare professional experienced in HIV treatment.  Dosage regimens varied significantly based on the individual patient's health status, other medications being taken, and the overall treatment plan which involved other antiretrovirals.  It was available in tablet and oral solution forms.  Any attempt to use this medication should be guided solely by a doctor familiar with its historical use and the current landscape of HIV/AIDS treatment.

### How it Works
Zalcitabine is metabolized in the body to become dideoxycytidine 5'-triphosphate (ddCTP).  This molecule acts as a competitive inhibitor of the HIV reverse transcriptase enzyme. It competes with the natural building block, deoxycytidine 5'-triphosphate (dCTP), preventing the incorporation of the natural building block into the growing viral DNA chain. This ultimately inhibits the replication of the HIV virus.  It also inhibits cellular DNA polymerase-beta and mitochondrial DNA polymerase-gamma, which highlights potential side effects related to cellular damage.

### Precautions
Given its discontinuation, using zalcitabine should only be considered under extremely limited and specific circumstances, and only under the guidance of a physician highly experienced in treating HIV.  Crucial precautions include:

*   **Contraindications:**  Individuals with pre-existing liver or pancreatic conditions should generally avoid this drug.
*   **Drug Interactions:** Zalcitabine may interact with other medications, particularly other antiretrovirals.  A doctor must carefully manage any potential drug interactions.
*   **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** The use of zalcitabine during pregnancy and breastfeeding requires careful consideration and monitoring by a healthcare professional due to the potential risks to the mother and the developing child.
*   **Elderly and Children:**  The use in elderly patients or children should be managed very carefully due to the potential for increased sensitivity to side effects.

### FAQs

*   **Q: Why was zalcitabine discontinued?** A: Its primary downfall was the rapid emergence of viral resistance when used as a monotherapy (alone). Newer and more effective antiretroviral regimens have significantly reduced the need for zalcitabine.
