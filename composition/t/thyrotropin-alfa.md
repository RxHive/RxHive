# Thyrotropin Alfa
### Generic Name
Thyrotropin Alfa

### Usage
Thyrotropin alfa is a synthetic version of thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH).  Its primary use is as a diagnostic aid in patients with well-differentiated thyroid cancer who have undergone thyroidectomy (surgical removal of the thyroid gland). It's used to stimulate the production of thyroglobulin (Tg), a protein produced by thyroid tissue.  Measuring Tg levels after Thyrotropin alfa administration helps detect any remaining thyroid tissue that might indicate cancer recurrence.  Thyrotropin alfa is also used to help with radioiodine ablation, a procedure to destroy any remaining thyroid tissue after surgery. This is particularly helpful in patients with well-differentiated thyroid cancer who haven't shown evidence of distant metastasis (cancer spread).

### Dosage
Thyrotropin alfa is administered intramuscularly (IM) into the buttock.  Dosage varies depending on the purpose:

* **Diagnostic Imaging:** 0.9 mg, followed by a second 0.9 mg dose 24 hours later. A serum thyroglobulin (Tg) sample is taken 72 hours after the second injection.  Radioactive iodine is usually administered 24 hours after the second injection, with imaging occurring 48 hours after that (72 hours after the second Thyrotropin alfa injection).

* **Radioiodine Ablation (Adjunctive):**  The same dosage as for diagnostic imaging (0.9 mg followed by 0.9 mg 24 hours later) is used.


**Pediatric Dosage:** The safety and effectiveness of Thyrotropin alfa in children have not been established.

**Dosage Adjustments:**  No specific dosage adjustments are recommended for patients with hepatic (liver) impairment. For those with renal (kidney) impairment, particularly those on dialysis, elimination is slower, and elevated TSH levels may persist longer.  Close monitoring is advised.

### Side Effects
**Common Side Effects:**

* Nausea
* Headache
* Dizziness
* Fatigue
* Vomiting
* Weakness

**Less Common, but Serious Side Effects:**

* Altered thyroid hormone levels (increased)
* Stroke (with or without typical symptoms)
* Hypersensitivity reactions (e.g., rash, itching, difficulty breathing)
* Injection site reactions (e.g., pain, redness)
* Transient flu-like symptoms


If you experience any side effects, especially those that are severe or persistent, consult your healthcare provider immediately.

### How it Works
Thyrotropin alfa mimics the action of naturally occurring TSH.  TSH stimulates the thyroid gland to produce and release thyroid hormones and thyroglobulin (Tg). By administering Thyrotropin alfa, doctors can boost Tg levels, making it easier to detect any remaining thyroid tissue through blood tests. In the case of radioiodine ablation, the stimulated thyroid tissue absorbs the radioactive iodine, leading to its destruction.


### Precautions
* **Contraindications:** Thyrotropin alfa is contraindicated in patients with a known hypersensitivity to the drug or any of its components. It's also contraindicated during pregnancy and breastfeeding when used with radioiodine.

* **Interactions:** No significant drug interactions are currently reported.

* **Warnings:**  Thyrotropin alfa can increase the risk of hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid), stroke, and rapid enlargement of any remaining thyroid tissue or metastases.  Caution is advised in patients with heart disease or those undergoing dialysis.

* **Specific Populations:**  Pregnant or breastfeeding women should not use Thyrotropin alfa with radioiodine. The decision to breastfeed after diagnostic testing without radioiodine should be made carefully, weighing the risks and benefits.  Use in children hasn't been studied.
