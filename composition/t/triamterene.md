# Triamterene

**Generic Name:** Triamterene

**Usage:** Triamterene is a potassium-sparing diuretic.  It's primarily used to treat:

* **High blood pressure (hypertension):** Often used in combination with other medications, particularly thiazide diuretics, to enhance blood pressure lowering effects while minimizing potassium loss.
* **Fluid retention (edema):** Associated with conditions like heart failure or cirrhosis of the liver.  Again, often used in combination with other diuretics.

**Side Effects:**  Like all medications, triamterene can cause side effects, although not everyone experiences them.  Common side effects include:

* **Nausea and vomiting**
* **Diarrhea or constipation**
* **Headache**
* **Dizziness**
* **Muscle weakness or cramps**
* **Increased thirst**
* **Frequent urination**

Less common, but more serious side effects include:

* **Hyperkalemia (high potassium levels in the blood):** This is a particularly serious side effect, especially in patients with kidney problems. Symptoms can include muscle weakness, irregular heartbeat, and even cardiac arrest.
* **Kidney stones**
* **Blood disorders**
* **Allergic reactions (rash, itching, swelling)**

**How it Works:** Triamterene works by preventing the kidneys from reabsorbing sodium and water. This leads to increased excretion of sodium and water in the urine, reducing blood volume and blood pressure.  Importantly, unlike many other diuretics, it also helps the kidneys retain potassium, minimizing the risk of hypokalemia (low potassium).  It achieves this by blocking sodium channels in the distal tubules of the kidneys.

Certain interactions, particularly with other potassium-sparing diuretics or potassium supplements, can lead to dangerously high potassium levels.
