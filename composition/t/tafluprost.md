# Tafluprost

**Generic Name:** Tafluprost

**Usage:**

Tafluprost is a prostaglandin analog used to treat open-angle glaucoma and ocular hypertension (high eye pressure).  It lowers intraocular pressure (IOP) by increasing the outflow of aqueous humor from the eye.  It's typically administered as eye drops once daily in the affected eye(s).  The specific dosage and frequency should always be determined by an ophthalmologist.

**Side Effects:**

Common side effects can include:

* **Eye irritation:** Burning, stinging, itching, redness, and dryness of the eyes. These are often mild and temporary.
* **Changes in eyelash appearance:**  Darkening, lengthening, and thickening of eyelashes (this is often considered a cosmetic side effect).
* **Eyelid changes:**  Eyelid skin darkening (hyperpigmentation).
* **Conjunctival hyperemia:** Redness of the conjunctiva (the membrane lining the inside of the eyelids and covering the white part of the eye).
* **Headache**


Less common, but more serious side effects can include:

* **Macular edema:** Swelling of the macula (the central part of the retina responsible for sharp, central vision).
* **Iritis/uveitis:** Inflammation of the iris (the colored part of the eye) or the uvea (the middle layer of the eye).
* **Hypotension:** Low blood pressure (although rare with topical application).
* **Allergic reactions:** These are rare but can include rash, itching, and swelling.


**How it Works:**

Tafluprost is a prostaglandin F2Î± analog.  It works primarily by increasing the outflow of aqueous humor from the eye through the uveoscleral pathway. Aqueous humor is the fluid that fills the space between the iris and the cornea, maintaining the eye's shape and providing nutrients. By increasing its outflow, tafluprost reduces the pressure inside the eye.  The exact mechanism isn't fully understood, but it's believed to involve the activation of specific receptors in the trabecular meshwork and uveoscleral tissues.
