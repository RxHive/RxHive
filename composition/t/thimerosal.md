# Thimerosal
### Generic Name
Thimerosal (also known as Thiomersal)

### Usage
Thimerosal is an organomercury compound primarily used as a preservative in various pharmaceutical products.  Historically, it was a common preservative in vaccines, immunoglobulins, nasal sprays, and contact lens solutions. However, due to public concerns, its use in childhood vaccines has been largely discontinued in the USA and European Union.  While it is no longer widely used in these applications, Thimerosal might still be found in some specialized medical products.  Its use in topical applications (like antiseptic solutions or ointments) was discontinued due to concerns about its toxicity and lack of effectiveness compared to safer alternatives.


### Dosage
There is no standard dosage of Thimerosal for human consumption or topical application as it's not intended for therapeutic use in the way a typical drug is.  Its concentration varies significantly depending on the specific pharmaceutical product it is used to preserve. The concentration in vaccines, for instance, was typically quite low.  Dosage information, if needed, would be found on the specific product label where it is listed as an ingredient.  This should not be self-administered; it is only used by pharmaceutical manufacturers during the production of their products.


### Side Effects
* **Local reactions:**  At the injection site (in the context of vaccines), some individuals may experience mild inflammation, redness, or swelling. These are generally considered mild and self-limiting.
* **Allergic reactions:** While rare, allergic reactions, ranging from mild skin rashes to more severe anaphylaxis, are possible.  This is a critical concern and necessitates immediate medical attention.
* **Neurotoxicity:**  Concerns about the potential neurotoxicity of Thimerosal, particularly in infants and young children, have led to its decreased use in vaccines. While the FDA has reviewed this and found no evidence of harm from the amounts used in vaccines *except for local reactions*,  further research continues to monitor potential long-term effects.


**Note:** If you experience any unusual or concerning side effects after receiving a medication containing Thimerosal, seek immediate medical advice.


### How it Works
Thimerosal's precise mechanism of action isn't fully understood.  It's believed to work by binding to sulfhydryl groups (-SH) present in various enzymes and proteins. This interaction interferes with the function of these molecules, likely contributing to its preservative effect by inhibiting microbial growth.  Additionally, it is believed to affect calcium channels, further influencing cellular processes.  However, its antimicrobial mechanism is more complex than simply inhibiting specific enzymes.


### Precautions
* **Allergy:** Individuals with a known allergy to mercury or thiomersal should avoid products containing this preservative.
* **Pregnancy and breastfeeding:**  While the FDA has not found conclusive evidence of harm from the amount of Thimerosal used in vaccines, pregnant or breastfeeding women should consult with their healthcare provider regarding the use of products containing Thimerosal.
* **Children:**  Due to past concerns about neurotoxicity, Thimerosal is no longer used in many childhood vaccines.
