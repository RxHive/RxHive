# Trioxasalen

**Generic Name:** Trioxsalen

**Usage:** Trioxsalen is a photosensitizing drug used primarily in the treatment of psoriasis and vitiligo.  It's applied topically or taken orally, always followed by exposure to specific wavelengths of ultraviolet (UV) light (usually UVA). This combined treatment is known as PUVA (psoralen plus ultraviolet A) therapy.  The exact usage and dosage depend on the condition being treated and the physician's judgment.

**Side Effects:** Trioxsalen's side effects can be significant and vary depending on the dosage and the duration of treatment.  They include:

* **Skin reactions:** Burning, itching, redness, dryness, scaling, blistering, and increased sensitivity to sunlight.  These are common and often temporary.  Severe reactions like phytophotodermatitis (plant-induced skin reaction) are possible.
* **Eye problems:** Eye irritation, burning, and increased sensitivity to light are common. More serious issues such as cataracts and retinal damage can occur with prolonged use, emphasizing the crucial need for eye protection during and after treatment.
* **Nausea and vomiting:** Gastrointestinal upset is a possibility, especially with oral administration.
* **Headache:** This is a relatively common side effect.
* **Pigmentation changes:**  Skin may become more pigmented (tanner) or less pigmented (lighter) in certain areas, especially with long-term use.  This can be unpredictable and sometimes permanent.
* **Premature aging of the skin:** Long-term PUVA therapy may accelerate skin aging.
* **Increased risk of skin cancer:** This is a serious long-term risk, particularly with frequent or prolonged PUVA therapy.  Regular skin checks are crucial.


**How it Works:** Trioxsalen is a psoralen, a type of furocoumarin.  When taken or applied topically, it absorbs into the skin. Upon exposure to UVA light, it binds to the DNA in skin cells, forming a complex that interferes with cell growth and division. In psoriasis, this slows the rapid growth of skin cells. In vitiligo, it may stimulate repigmentation.  The exact mechanisms are still under investigation.
