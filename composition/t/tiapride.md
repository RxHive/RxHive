# Tiapride

**Generic Name:** Tiapride

**Usage:**

Tiapride is an antipsychotic medication primarily used to treat:

* **Chronic extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) in Parkinson's disease:**  It's particularly effective in managing motor fluctuations (on-off phenomenon) and dyskinesia in patients with Parkinson's disease already receiving levodopa therapy.  It's *not* a first-line treatment for Parkinson's itself.
* **Tardive dyskinesia (TD):** Although its effectiveness is debated, it may be considered in treating this condition, characterized by involuntary movements, often a side effect of other antipsychotic medications.  It's not a first-line treatment for TD either.
* **Other uses:** In some regions, it may be used off-label for other conditions, but this is less common and should only be done under strict medical supervision.

**Important Note:** Tiapride's use is generally restricted to specific situations and populations due to its side effect profile and the availability of alternative treatments with potentially better safety profiles.


**Side Effects:**

Common side effects can include:

* **Neurological effects:**  These are the most significant concern.  They can include akathisia (restlessness), dystonia (muscle spasms), parkinsonism (symptoms similar to Parkinson's disease), and potentially worsening of existing EPS.
* **Gastrointestinal effects:** Nausea, vomiting, constipation.
* **Cardiovascular effects:** Orthostatic hypotension (a sudden drop in blood pressure upon standing), palpitations.
* **Other possible side effects:** Drowsiness, dizziness, fatigue, weight changes, dry mouth, blurred vision.

Rare but serious side effects can include:

* **Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS):** A rare but life-threatening condition characterized by high fever, muscle rigidity, altered consciousness, and autonomic instability.
* **Sudden cardiac death:** Although rare, this is a potential risk.


**How it Works:**

Tiapride is a dopamine D2 receptor antagonist, meaning it blocks the action of dopamine in the brain.  However, unlike many other typical antipsychotics, it has a relatively selective action on dopamine D2 receptors and does *not* significantly affect other neurotransmitter systems like serotonin.  This selective action is thought to contribute to its usefulness in managing EPS in Parkinson's disease, although the precise mechanism isn't fully understood.  It is believed that by modulating dopaminergic activity, it helps to reduce the severity of involuntary movements associated with EPS and TD.
