# Tacrolimus

**Generic Name:** Tacrolimus

**Usage:**

Tacrolimus is an immunosuppressant drug primarily used to prevent organ rejection after transplantation (kidney, liver, heart, lung, bone marrow).  It's also sometimes used to treat severe autoimmune diseases like atopic dermatitis (eczema) that haven't responded to other treatments.  The specific dosage and administration route (oral or intravenous) depend on the condition being treated and the patient's individual needs.  It's crucial to follow the prescribed dosage and schedule precisely.

**How it Works:**

Tacrolimus works by inhibiting calcineurin, an enzyme involved in the activation of T-lymphocytes, a type of white blood cell crucial for the immune response. By suppressing the activity of these cells, tacrolimus reduces the body's ability to reject transplanted organs and also helps to control the overactive immune response in autoimmune diseases.

* **Q: How long do I need to take tacrolimus?**  A: The duration of treatment varies greatly depending on the condition and individual response. For organ transplantation, it's often a long-term treatment, potentially for life. For autoimmune diseases, the duration might be shorter, but it's determined by the physician.

* **Q: What should I avoid while taking tacrolimus?** A:  Avoid grapefruit and grapefruit juice, as they can interfere with the metabolism of tacrolimus, leading to higher blood levels and increased risk of side effects.  Inform your doctor about all other medications you are taking, including over-the-counter drugs and herbal supplements, as interactions are possible.

* **Q: What are the signs of a serious side effect?** A:  Seek immediate medical attention if you experience severe side effects such as chest pain, shortness of breath, severe abdominal pain, changes in urine output, significant swelling, signs of infection (fever, chills), or any neurological symptoms like seizures or confusion.

* **Q: Can I drink alcohol while taking tacrolimus?** A:  Generally, it's advisable to limit or avoid alcohol while on tacrolimus, as alcohol can worsen some side effects, particularly liver damage. Discuss alcohol consumption with your doctor.

* **Q: Can I stop taking tacrolimus abruptly?** A:  No, never stop taking tacrolimus abruptly without consulting your doctor.  Stopping suddenly can lead to organ rejection (in transplant patients) or a flare-up of autoimmune disease.  The dosage must be tapered off gradually under medical supervision.
