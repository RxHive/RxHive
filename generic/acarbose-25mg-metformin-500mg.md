**Usage:** This combination medication is used to treat type 2 diabetes. It's typically prescribed for patients whose blood sugar levels aren't adequately controlled by metformin alone.  Acarbose helps slow down the breakdown of carbohydrates in the gut, while metformin improves insulin sensitivity and decreases glucose production by the liver. The combination aims to achieve better blood sugar control.

**Side Effects:** Side effects can vary from person to person, but common side effects for this combination include:

* **Gastrointestinal issues:** These are the most frequent and can include diarrhea, bloating, gas, abdominal pain, and nausea. These are more commonly associated with acarbose.

* **Other potential side effects:** Headaches, dizziness, and fatigue are also possible.

**How it Works:**

* **Acarbose:** This is an alpha-glucosidase inhibitor. It works by delaying the digestion and absorption of carbohydrates in the small intestine. This leads to a slower rise in blood glucose levels after meals.

* **Metformin:** This is a biguanide. It works through multiple mechanisms to lower blood glucose levels.  It reduces glucose production by the liver, improves insulin sensitivity in the muscles and fat tissue, and may slightly reduce glucose absorption in the intestines.

The combination of acarbose and metformin leverages the complementary mechanisms of these two drugs for improved glycemic control.
